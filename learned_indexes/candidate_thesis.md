## 2. Design razors (principles I keep rediscovering)

- **R1 — The hard ε-bound is non-negotiable.** It's the one thing nobody else has. The moment a key is allowed past ε "temporarily," I'm ALEX with extra steps. *Defer the optimization, never the guarantee.*
- **R2 — Does this couple the pieces?** Disjoint segments are independent → local split/merge works + free lemma holds. Continuity / cross-piece optimization / shared knots *couple* pieces → fight locality. A *filter*, not a veto, but disjoint is the default for a reason.
- **R3 — Structural guarantee > hope-the-tuning-is-right.** The recurring failure of ALEX (cost model) and LIPP (bulk adjustment + global `G`) is a guarantee contingent on heuristics/background work. Prefer invariants.
- **R4 — Don't over-invest in the fit.** Segmentation is solved (PGM optimal) / near-solved (RS cheap) / provably-hard-to-improve (free knots). My contribution is the *update mechanism*, not the fit.
- **R5 — ε bounds *positions*, not *latency*.** Keep ε as the invariant I *prove*; treat latency as the evidence I *measure*. Don't claim ε solves wall-clock time.
- **R6 — Space-honest.** Compare at *equal memory* (the ALEX-M lesson). Don't let gaps/slack inflate a fake win.
- **R7 — count = latency only while pieces are uniform-cost.** Per-segment `G` (different eval costs) breaks "min segments = min latency." Watch this if I add `G`.
- **R8 — deterministic guaranteed maintenance, not cost-model maintenance.** Traditional index *structures* (B+-tree, ART, hash) maintain themselves with *fixed deterministic rules that carry worst-case guarantees* (node full → split in half → provably balanced). They avoid cost models for self-maintenance *precisely because* cost models trade the guarantee away. Cost models in DBs are 45 years old — but they live at the **query-optimizer layer** (Selinger/System R), NOT at the index-structure-maintenance layer. ALEX/LIFT did something relatively novel by pushing a cost model *down into structural maintenance* — and that's exactly what I push back on. **My identity: learned *prediction* (the model, for space/speed) + traditional-style *deterministic guaranteed maintenance* (ε-violation → split, underflow → merge).** I bring the B+-tree's maintenance philosophy to the learned index's prediction. The sharpest contrast with LIFT isn't just "hard bound vs tuned knob" — it's **deterministic guaranteed maintenance vs cost-model maintenance.** Same fork, maintenance angle.

**⚠️ These trade off — I can't maximize all of them.** Deepest tensions: **slack/gaps (cheap in-place updates) vs density (space + fast scans)**, and **rigid/simple (concurrency + predictable writes) vs adaptive/clever (performance + robustness)**.

| I want… | Why (and who showed me) | Tradeoff — what it restricts |
|---|---|---|
| **Hard worst-case ε-bound** | Predictability + robustness under drift; the 2022–25 reckoning says this is *the* missing thing. PGM has it; ALEX/LIPP don't. | Bounds *positions* not latency; pay for it on *every* key; tighter ε → more segments; a fixed bound can't *adapt* to workload. |
| **In-place / no read amplification** | PGM's shelf taxes *every read* `log n`. Want updates that don't scatter data. | Forces **pointers** (lose cache) **or gapped arrays** (lose space + scan tax) **or rebuild** (the shelf). Also makes **concurrency harder**. |

| **Space-honest / small** | LIPP's lookup win is partly a *space* trade (ALEX-M beats it). | Small space **fights gaps** (needed for in-place), hot/cold redundancy, insert slack. |

**Over-constrained meta-point:** "in-place + pointer-free + dense + good-scans" can't all max out. My job is *choosing where on these tradeoffs to sit*, with the ε-bound (R1) as the one fixed point. Everything else is a dial.

**Ordered scan — who has it:** PGM **best** (contiguous array, pointer-free) · B+-tree great (sibling-linked leaves) · ALEX good-but-gap-taxed (loses past ~1000-key scans) · LIPP **poor** (data scattered across all levels). *Design note: keep gaps in the segment index, data dense → PGM-class scans; gapping data reinherits ALEX's tax.*

## 7. Core mechanism (leading candidate — not decided)

> *Direction, not commitment.* Keep ALEX's local in-place split, but every split **preserves a hard ε bound** (trigger = ε-violation *or* ε-proximity per I5; cut = where segmentation forces it), over a **pointer-free gapped segment array**, with a **local B+-tree-style merge** (not LIPP's bulk rebuild) as balancer. = "ALEX's locality + PGM's guarantee + B+-tree's predictable rebalancing, distribution-agnostic."

**Why feasible — the free lemma (I1).** Sub-range monotonicity (PGM §2.1): any sub-range of an ε-valid segment is ε-valid → splitting at *any* interior point keeps both halves ε-valid **for free**. "Does splitting preserve the bound?" is already *yes*.
**Precision on "free" — don't overclaim it.** The lemma covers sub-ranges of an *already-valid* segment. So a **proactive** split (I5, cutting a still-valid segment) is *literally fit-free* — both halves inherit the parent model. A **violation-triggered** split fires *because a new key broke ε*, and that key is *not* in the original valid range → the free lemma doesn't cover it → the receiving half needs a real but **local, O(segment-size)** re-fit. **Clean splits free; dirty splits cheap-and-local.**
**Insert cost spectrum (= the real meaning of "no retrain").** Gap-hit + still within ε → *zero* model work (the common case); threatens ε but one line still covers → re-fit *one* segment; breaks ε → split → fit *two* segments; proactive cut → free. Fit cost is bounded by a single segment's size, **never by `n`.** "We don't retrain" means *never a global retrain* — only bounded local re-fit on O(1) segments.
**Two guarantees — do not conflate them.** (1) *Each op is cheap & local* — basically follows once ops are segment-scoped → **table stakes (ALEX has it too).** (2) *Segment count stays ≤ c·m under churn* — the hard, open part → **the contribution.** Cheap-per-op is the premise that makes the question interesting; the count bound is the answer that makes it a thesis.

**Hard Part 1 — layout (I4): pointer-free + in-place = the empty cell.**
*Why pointer-free at all (mechanism, not slogan):* a pointer is an arbitrary address → following it is a **dependent load** (can't compute the next address until this one returns) → a tree descent is a chain of dependent cache misses (~80–100ns each, serial, unprefetchable), cost `depth × miss`. **Computed routing** replaces the *read* of the next address with a *multiply-add on the key* (~1ns, in-register), with one memory touch at the very end. That resolves the `log²N`-beats-`logN` paradox: big-O counts steps, but a pointer step is a 100ns stall and a computed step a 1ns flop → a computed structure can do *more work* and still win wall-clock. Bonus: smaller (floats, not 8-byte pointers → more index in cache) + SIMD/prefetch-friendly. **Honest cost (R5):** pointer-free *demands* a contiguous, position-addressable layout → fights in-place mutation AND concurrency (no pointer to CAS-swap → LIFT's RCU story doesn't transfer; I'd need versioned slots over the array). A read-side, hardware-empirical bet, not a theorem.
*Is "pointer-free AND in-place" even possible? Yes — decouple three layers.* **(1) As abstract properties: settled.** The **Packed Memory Array (PMA)** (Itai–Konheim–Rodeh 1981 → cache-oblivious B-trees) is *already* pointer-free, in-place, sorted, position-addressable, with a **proven amortized O(log²n) element-moves/update** + cache-optimal scans. A ~40-year-old witness; not a square circle. **(2) As my structure: engineering risk, not scientific** — "PGM's computed *hierarchical* routing, each level a PMA instead of a static array." Every part exists independently (computed routing = PGM; mutable pointer-free sorted array w/ proof = PMA; model→gapped-mutable-array = ALEX data nodes, pointer-free *within* a node). Unassembled because static-pointer-free people (PGM) reached for the shelf and dynamic people (ALEX/LIFT) reached for pointers — **path-dependence, not impossibility.** **(3) As the *guaranteed* structure: OPEN — that's Hard Part 2.** ⚠️ Don't let (2)'s buildability fool you into thinking (3) is done; don't let (3)'s openness scare you off (2). They're decoupled — build with confidence, prove with humility.
*The diagonal (why the cell looks empty):* pointer-free papers (PGM, RadixSpline, RMI) are **static or shelf-dynamic**; every **in-place** paper (ALEX, LIPP, LIFT) uses **pointers.** Mutation normally *forces* pointers; I'm refusing → the gapped array is what I pay *instead of* pointers to occupy the cell. ⭐ **Use PMA, not hand-rolled gaps** — it's the version with an analyzable maintenance bound, and its density-window rebalancing analysis is a **second proof template alongside Graefe.** Cost either way: gap space (quantify); leverage: `m ≪ n` so the tax lands on the *small* segment array (shift = O(m), not O(n)).

**Hard Part 2 — THE theorem (I2+I3): what's *proved* vs what's *open*.** Local splits drift you off optimal `m` (boundaries land where *inserts* forced them, not where a global sweep would). The goal was **never `M = m`** — it's **`M ≤ c·m`** (bounded drift), the same bargain as a B-tree (never optimally packed, just ≥ half-full → ≤ 2× optimal node count). Giving up exact `m` isn't failure; it's the *price* that buys locality, and the bound is the proof the price is capped.

**The invariant = *irreducibility*.** A segment doesn't underflow by having *too few keys* (a tiny segment may optimally cover a high-curvature region). It underflows when **a single ε-line covers it *together with* a neighbor** → "redundant," not "empty." Merge = collapse such a pair. *Irreducible* = no adjacent pair is jointly ε-coverable. (Note: irreducible ⊊ optimal — pairwise-merge-checking asks "can these two collapse to one?", never "can these three re-cut into two?"; merge removes a boundary, it can't *slide* one. So irreducible is the *achievable local* target, strictly weaker than `m`.)

**Static lemma (the EASY half — falls out for free): an irreducible segmentation has `M ≤ 2m − 1`.** Charging argument off the free lemma: no optimal segment `Oᵢ` can fully contain *two* of mine (their union ⊆ `Oᵢ` is coverable by the free lemma → they'd have merged) → ≤ m of mine are "interior"; every other straddles one of the m−1 optimal boundaries → ≤ m−1 straddlers → **M ≤ 2m−1, so `c = 2`.** ⚡ *The free lemma does double duty — it makes splits free **and** bounds the count.* Scale-free: no `n` in it (insert a billion keys, `m` grows, the 2× ratio holds). ⚠️ **Originality flag:** this is *standard-flavored* (competitive analysis of interval covering) → **lit-check before claiming**; treat as **foundation-to-cite**, not the contribution.

**The OPEN part (the hard half — the contribution): can I *maintain* irreducibility under arbitrary insert AND delete with *bounded amortized work*** (no adversary-forced merge cascades)? The static lemma is conditional — "*if* irreducible, *then* ≤ 2m" — so **space is safe *while the invariant holds*, even adversarially**; the adversary's leverage is on the **work to hold it**, not the space. This is the genuinely-open, B-tree-balance-grade theorem and the whole game. Even a *negative* result (adversary forces M/m unbounded under any local merge policy → need non-local merge) is publishable. (ALEX never implemented merge; LIPP merged only in bulk → "nobody did this local + bounded.")

**Deletes = the open frontier (the subtle direction).** *Validity is delete-safe for free:* a subset of an ε-valid set is ε-valid → **a delete never breaks ε, never forces a split.** But deletes attack *optimality silently:* an insert that hurts is **loud** (breaks ε → fires → split). A delete that hurts is **silent** — it thins segments until a neighbor pair becomes mergeable, but **nothing fires** → you're reducible (M > 2m) and *not told.* So deletes cost a **detection** mechanism, not just a repair one, and the merge trigger can't be a B-tree fill-factor count — it must be the **ε-coverability test against neighbors** (strictly more work; exactly what ALEX skipped and LIPP did only in bulk). *Carry into Graefe:* his amortized split/merge accounting bounds the cost of reconciling silent shrinkage — port the *machinery*, not the (fill-factor) trigger.

**`c` is policy-set, not nature.** Eager-merge + reactive split → `c = 2`. Proactive split (I5) leaves deliberate redundancy + deferred merge (I6) runs a backlog → **`c = 2 + f(laziness, proactivity)`.** *Quantifying that trade is itself a publishable result.* So the sharpest statement of the contribution: **the space bound is free (`c=2` via the free lemma); the work is the theorem (cheaply maintain irreducibility under adversarial churn); and characterizing how I5/I6 move `c` off 2 is the bonus result.**

**⭐ Boundary displacement — the sharper cascade lemma (this *protects* the whole `c·m` choice).** *Segmentation-theory grounding (researched):* exact minimum `m` is solved **statically** in O(N) (Imai–Iri 1986; Hakimi–Schmeichel 1991) and **streaming-optimal** via two convex hulls (OptimalPLR — Xie et al., VLDB-J 2014) — and the optimal streaming method **is greedy** (longest-valid-segment, then restart). Subtlety to file: greedy with a **floating** line = optimal; greedy with a **pinned** origin (ShrinkingCone) = *sub*optimal — and the pinned cone is exactly what FITing-Tree chose (they called optimal "impractical," but OptimalPLR is also O(N), so the real cost was floating-hull vs pinned-cone, not greedy-vs-optimal). **Appends stay exactly optimal incrementally, O(1) amortized** (streaming) → my append fast-path is optimal *for free*. **But mid-range edits do NOT admit cheap exact-`m` maintenance:** the optimal partition is a global min-link object, and one mid-range insert can force a **re-segmentation cascade** that re-flows boundaries non-locally (the *justified-paragraph line-break* analogy: insert one word near the top → every later line can re-break → Θ(n) boundaries move, worst case; deletes mirror it). A **fully dynamic convex hull** (Reinstädtler–Rotenberg, O(log n·log log n) amortized) maintains the **hull**, not the **partition** — it's a faster primitive *inside* a re-segmentation, not a substitute for it. PGM's answer is to not maintain it (rebuild via the shelf = the read-amp I reject).

**The open question (= a sharper cascade-bounding lemma).** Define an edit's **boundary displacement** = how many *optimal* boundaries move when you insert/delete one key and recompute the optimum. Worst-case = Θ(n). **OPEN: what is it *amortized*?** Plausibly O(1)/O(polylog), splay-flavored — a long cascade can only fire after slack accumulates, and consuming it leaves future edits cheap. This is my cascade-bounding lemma pointed at a **harder object**: my lemma bounds boundary *removal* in the 2-competitive structure (→ "`2m` maintained cheaply"); the sharper version bounds boundary *movement* in the **exact-optimal** partition (→ "`≈ m` maintained cheaply"). Removal-only is a *strict sub-case* of slide-included, so bounding the harder object buys strictly more.

**Both outcomes publish — and that's the point.** *Upper bound* (small amortized displacement) → re-derive only the O(polylog) moved boundaries per edit (dynamic hull as the primitive) → maintain `≈ m`, strictly better than `2m`. *Lower bound* (an adversary forcing Θ(n) amortized displacement) → **proves exact `m` cannot be cheaply maintained → converts `c·m` from "a pragmatic choice" into "provably necessary,"** so a referee *cannot* ask "why not just maintain optimal?" ⚠️ **Honest weighting:** detecting *which* boundaries move and *which* edits cascade is itself machinery, and combined with silent-delete detection it may cost more than the 2× space it saves → **weight this as the lower-bound that SHIELDS `c·m`, not an upper bound to chase.** Pragmatic target stays `2m` via local merge; this question protects that target rather than replacing it. *(It also explains why irreducibility is the right relaxation: merge-on-underflow only ever **removes** a redundant boundary — it never **slides** one, which is the cascade-prone move. The `2×` is precisely the price of refusing to chase the moving optimal boundaries.)*

**⭐⭐ Deletion → restore-irreducibility: the worked proof plan (discharges the detection + cascade-bounding lemmas).** *Researched the actual machinery — the literature gives every piece; nobody has assembled them for deletion-driven re-optimization of a learned-index segmentation with a hard ε → the contribution is the assembly + one proof.* **Reframe:** "keep 2m−1" = **restore irreducibility** (the 2-approx), NOT re-optimize to true `m`. Only ever *remove* a redundant boundary; never *slide* one (the cascade-prone boundary-displacement move). That restriction is what makes it provable.

- **Step 1 — Detection is local & free (resolves the detection lemma on the delete side).** Deleting key `k` from segment `S` only shrinks `S`'s point set → cannot change coverability of any pair *not* involving `S` → the **only** newly-mergeable pairs are `(L,S)` and `(S,R)`. **Detection per delete = exactly 2 coverability tests, no global scan.** (Deletion's locality hands you detection; the silent-asymmetry is *only* that deletes don't fire, so you *run* the 2 checks instead of waiting for a violation.)
- **Step 2 — Coverability test = x-disjoint convex-hull bridge-merge.** "Is `L∪S` ε-coverable by one line?" = OptimalPLR cone-nonempty check over the union. Adjacent segments are **x-disjoint** (all keys of `L` < all of `S`) → merging their hulls is a single **bridge/tangent** op (Overmars–van Leeuwen). Hull-tree per segment → **O(log) per test**; recent 3-case bridge test (van der Hoog / Reinstädtler–Rotenberg 2026) makes it implementable.
- **Step 3 — Merge reuses the test output.** The bridge-merge *is* the merged hull; the feasible line reads off the cone → **no re-fit from scratch** (≈ O(log); merge keeps using the existing hull structures).
- **Step 4 — Cascade amortization = the cascade-bounding lemma (B-slack template).** After `L∪S→M`, `M` may be coverable with its new left neighbor → cascade (same shape as a B-tree merge cascading to the root). Bound via **potential Φ = segment count `M`**: each delete creates ≤ O(1) merge-debt (≤ 2 mergeable pairs); each merge pays 1; each split adds O(1) → **total merges ≤ deletes + splits + init → amortized O(1) merges/delete**, each O(log) → **amortized O(log)/delete, `M ≤ 2m−1` at every step** (eager). [Template: B-slack trees (amortized-O(1) rebalancing via potential); Graefe.]
- **🔑 The slack trick (the single most important proof decision).** Naive "merge whenever coverable" lets an adversary oscillate (delete→merge, insert→split, repeat). Fix = merge only with **margin**: coverable at `(1−δ)ε`, not at the ε edge → a fresh merged segment isn't one insert from re-splitting → breaks the oscillation. **This δ is exactly the `c = 2 + f(slack)` dial** — slack buys amortization for a looser constant. (= B-slack's "leave slack after compress so splits don't immediately re-occur.")

**The three regimes (each has a *known* bound → pick the risk):**
- **Eager** (restore irreducibility every delete): `M ≤ 2m−1` *always*; O(log)/delete *if* cascade amortization holds. The strong version.
- **Lazy / deferred (I6, background):** `M ≤ 2m−1 + backlog`; cheaper/delete, weaker instantaneous bound. [De-amortization template: Bε-tree — store large leaves separately + periodically split/merge selected leaves while updates arrive.]
- **No-rebalance + periodic global rebuild (Sen–Tarjan, "Deletion Without Rebalancing"):** O(1) amortized/delete, let segments thin, rebuild every Θ(N) ops, reset to `m`. ⚠️ Periodic rebuild = a bounded mini-shelf (the thing I avoid) — but cleanest analysis = the **safety net** if eager proves too hard.

**⚠️ The one open obligation (what's actually left to prove).** The potential bounds merge **count**; the adversary attacks merge **cost** — a long cascade re-merges over *growing* segments, and hull-JOIN is amortized but worst-case linear in merged size. **OPEN: prove the cascade *cost* (not count) stays bounded-amortized under the adversarial insert+delete mix.** Three ingredients to *compose*: B-slack potential (bounds cascade length) + hull-JOIN potential (bounds per-merge cost) + slack margin (kills oscillation). The frontier is now one focused lemma with its ingredients named.

**Citation cluster (deletes / cascade machinery):** Overmars–van Leeuwen 1981 (dynamic hull, O(log²n)); Hershberger–Suri 1992 (deletion-only hull); Chan 2001 / Brodal–Jacob 2002 (O(log^{1+ε}) / O(log n) dynamic hull); van der Hoog / Reinstädtler–Rotenberg 2026 (engineering fully-dynamic hull, 3-case bridge); B-slack trees (amortized-O(1) rebalancing potential); Sen–Tarjan, *Deletion Without Rebalancing in Multiway Search Trees* (O(1) amortized, no-rebalance + rebuild); Graefe (B-tree merge template — already core); Sim-Piece (Liakos et al., VLDB 2023 — segment merging for PLA); Bε-tree de-amortization (arXiv 2211.06044).

**⭐ Target tightness — `2m−1` is the locality sweet spot, NOT a compromise (settled decision + the re-optimization trap).** *Committee will probe "why not re-optimize to true `m`?" — here's the answer, and why the current plan is the right point.* **The deep reason the 2× exists:** optimal `m` is a **global** min-link object — the minimum-link partition is a single interlocking thing, and one mid-range edit can force the optimal boundaries to slide all down the line (the boundary-displacement cascade). Irreducibility only ever inspects **two neighbors at a time**. So the relaxation `m → 2m−1` is exactly the trade *global-and-expensive → local-and-cheap*: **the 2× is what buys locality, and locality is what makes both the maintenance cheap and the cascade proof tractable.** Not a concession — the enabling move.
- **The spectrum (ordered by how local the maintenance is):** (a) **optimal `m`** → *global* maintenance (boundary slides, non-local cascades) → expensive; (b) **`2m−1` eager** → *local* (adjacent-merge only), tight at every instant → **the plan**; (c) **`2m−1` + bounded backlog** → *deferred-local* (I6), cheapest per-op, slightly looser bound → de-amortization math (B-slack / Sen–Tarjan). *Tighter than 2m−1 costs locality (needs global info); looser just defers the same local work. `2m−1` eager is the unique point where maintenance is still fully local AND the bound is tight at every instant.*
- **Periodic re-optimization to true `m` = a thesis-trap.** No cheap version exists: `m` is global → re-optimizing to it = **full O(n) re-segmentation = PGM's shelf reintroduced**. Buys *nothing* on the theorem (`2m−1` and `m` are the same asymptotic class; the contribution is *bounded-at-all under adversarial churn*, not the constant) and **blurs the "no rebuild" identity** that separates me from PGM. ❌ Not in the thesis. ✅ Valid *only* as a **de-amortized background knob in a production build** (a factor-2 segment reduction genuinely shrinks space / shallows the tree) — never stop-the-world; spread as slow background re-segmentation so no latency spike.
- **The real "reduce rebalancing" lever is *eagerness, not tightness*.** I'm already collecting the tightness prize (going to 2m−1 is *what* made maintenance local). The remaining lever is lazy/deferred merge (I6): skip the cascade on most deletes + batch + background → less per-op work, priced as the bounded-backlog loosening. (Middle option if ever wanted: *windowed* re-opt over ~10 consecutive segments — between 2m−1 and m, cheaper than global, but a heuristic with no clean "=optimal" guarantee. Tuning knob, not theorem.)
- **🔵 Open tangent (bonus, not blocker):** is there a *local* rule beating `2m−1` (e.g. `1.5m`) while staying local? None known — irreducibility's 2× looks like the natural local optimum; beating it seems to need non-local info. A nice standalone result if found; the thesis stands without it.

**PGM does NOT already do this (vocabulary trap).** PGM's "merge" = an **LSM run-merge**: combine two doubling-size *runs* and **rebuild a static index from scratch** (Bentley–Saxe binary-counter; tombstone deletes; lookups search *all* levels → ×log n read-amp). Granularity = a level; purpose = *avoid* mutation. *My* "merge" = **local segment merge-on-underflow** (two neighbors collapse in place, O(segment), no rebuild). Opposite philosophies, same verb: PGM merges runs *so it never maintains segments*; I merge segments *so I never rebuild runs.* PGM never enters the irreducibility regime — it **discards & rebuilds to optimal-`m`** instead of maintaining. **So per-structure PGM has *fewer* segments than me (exact `m` < my 2m)** — its dividend for rebuilding. ⚠️ **I do NOT win on segment count.**

**⚠️ Eval discipline (or I hand PGM the win).** Benchmark on **read-amplification, tail latency, write-predictability** on *one dynamic structure* — the axes where not-rebuilding pays. **Never** on raw segment count on a static snapshot, where PGM's from-scratch optimality beats me by construction. (Pairs with the "evaluate where the invariant pays" ⚠️ in the hard-invariant decision below.)

**Global re-segmentation = optional, non-load-bearing ONLY.** It buys just the `2m → m` constant factor, at the price of an O(n) bulk pass = the LIPP/shelf tail-spike I reject (and it spends the predictable-tail-latency payoff). **Plan A (the real claim):** the local invariant alone holds ≤ 2m forever → *no global pass needed* → throw the rebuild away entirely. **Fallback only:** if the dynamic-maintenance proof fails, a periodic pass rescues space — but then the claim shrinks from "I proved local maintenance is bounded" to "I bounded it by occasionally rebuilding," half-rejoining the shelf camp I defined myself against. *Litmus (same shape as the ε-envelope): is the worst-case guarantee true at all times from the local invariant, or only right after a pass?* The latter = the shelf smuggled back in.

**Proactive ε-proximity splitting (I5) — how the split could *schedule*.** Split *before* ε breaks, when keys crowd the bound (e.g. max/p90 error > 0.8ε), NOT at 80% *density* (that's accidentally ALEX's `dᵤ` — the wrong currency; measure proximity to *ε*, the actual constraint). Why it's strong: (a) **never violates ε** — splits while still valid → hard bound intact, theorem clean; (b) **doesn't collapse to ALEX** — triggers on ε-proximity, a signal only a *bounded* index can read; (c) **smooths writes (A4)** — spreads split-work into calm periods; (d) **proactive** — the reactive→proactive frontier the field misses; (e) **ε-crowding is an early drift signal** (keys pressing the bound = local distribution drifting off the line) → split adapts *before* damage. Costs: more segments → **larger `c` in the bound** (real, tunable space-vs-predictability dial, parameterized by the threshold); a cheap per-segment max-error stat; a threshold knob — but a *principled* one (in ε-units, R3-friendlier than ALEX's constants). **Open Q: derive the threshold (e.g. from insert rate) rather than tune it.**

**Deferred merge (I6) — predictable writes without breaking R1.** Split *eagerly* (cheap, holds ε); defer only the **merge/rebalance** (the optimality-restoring, occasionally-cascading part) to a background pass. Guarantee always held; only *space-optimality* is eventually-consistent. Gives the tail-smoothing the 2ε idea wanted, without the guarantee-contingency that sank it (I10).

**Honest counter (R5).** ε bounds positions, not latency. Resolution: ε is the **invariant I prove**; latency is the **evidence I measure**.

**Other shapes kept open:** bound LIPP-style (precise positions + ε fallback), or full-PGM pointer-free everywhere. Holding open until I've read more.

**[DECISION] The ε-bound is a HARD INVARIANT — committed.** Not a tuned knob (LIFT), not emergent (ALEX): every key within ε *always, by construction*, under arbitrary updates. *Why this, not LIFT's cost-model (which works well):* the invariant buys what a cost model structurally can't — (1) **adversarial robustness** (LIFT defends poisoning attacks *empirically* = only tested inputs; an invariant holds against *any* input, incl. unimagined ones — exactly what "unpredictable future key distributions" demands); (2) **predictable tail latency** (bounded worst-case search per key, not just low average); (3) **composability** (a hard-bounded component can be a primitive in a guaranteed system; an empirically-robust one can't — *this is why the thesis matters beyond itself*). *Honest cost:* the invariant is NOT free — split eagerly, pay on every key, may be bigger/slower on *average-case* benchmarks. So I do NOT claim to beat LIFT on its turf (avg-case time-space tradeoff); I claim a turf LIFT *can't* occupy (provable worst-case robustness). **⚠️ Evaluate on the regime where the invariant pays (adversarial / tail-latency / worst-case), not just avg-case — else I make my own contribution look weak.** Razor #1 is now a hard *constraint*, not a goal: every design choice is asked "does this preserve the bound?"

**Hybrid door (held loosely — readings may surface a clean one).** A hybrid is legitimate iff: **cost-model the DIALS, never the GUARANTEE.** ε stays an invariant; everything it leaves *unconstrained* (gap/density, *when* to merge, hot/cold tiering) is fair game for LIFT-style cost-model tuning. Two natural forms: (a) hard ε on positions + cost-model on the free parameters; (b) hard ε as the floor (the PGM-floor safety net), cost-model picks *among* equally-ε-valid options for better average behavior. Any hybrid that makes the *bound itself* contingent on the cost model = the 2ε trap (collapses to ALEX, set-aside in the Ledger). Any hybrid that optimizes *within* the bound = fine.

**[DECISION] How ε's *value* is chosen — auto-derived from a budget, frozen; runtime cost-model only inside a fixed envelope.** ε must NOT be a user-typed magic number, and the way I avoid that must not smuggle in LIFT's mistake. **Default (safe, probably what I want):** derive ε *once* at build from a **space/latency budget** (PGM §6 — power-law + root-find) and **freeze it**. A frozen constant leaves the `c·m` proof completely untouched — *the proof never cared who picked the constant, only that it's fixed.* No runtime cost model needed to escape "user-decided." **Optional adaptive mode (only if I want per-node / hot-cold ε):** a runtime cost model may pick ε **iff it picks from a fixed, workload-independent envelope `[ε_min, ε_max]`**, both endpoints constants set *before any input*. Then — the **litmus that decides everything** — *is the bound on the cost model a fixed constant, or a quantity the workload can move?*
- **Fixed envelope → proof survives**, but mind *what* is proven: the **guarantee** I can claim is the one at **ε_max** (loosest — an adversary can drive the model to its loose end); the **segment-count bound** is stressed at **ε_min** (tightest → most segments). Both endpoints fixed → both proofs hold. The cost model's choices *inside* the envelope are a **pure average-case optimization that cannot make the worst case worse than the envelope.** This is the concrete instance of the hybrid rule above, and it can *serve* hot/cold (A1): tight ε hot = fast lookups, loose ε cold = compact, all still bounded. So I'd be optimizing the average case *inside* a worst-case skeleton — both, in the right layers.
- **Moving envelope → fatal (= I10 / the 2ε trap).** The moment the cost model may *raise* ε_max because "splitting is too expensive right now," there is no fixed ceiling, the adversary inserts under pressure, the model keeps loosening ε to dodge the split, ε → unbounded → ALEX with extra steps. `pick ε ∈ [4,64]` (fixed) = safe; `pick ε to minimize current insert cost` (unbounded above) = cliff.

**Sharpest LIFT line (carries the whole differentiation):** LIFT picks ε via a cost model with *no fixed input-independent ceiling* — which is *exactly* why it has no worst-case bound. The **clamp to a fixed envelope is the one thing LIFT didn't do**, and it *is* the difference between "I bound the worst case" and "I'm LIFT." Adding a cost model for ε does not blur the fork *if* the clamp is a constant; it sharpens it. ✅ **Homework RESOLVED (full read, §4.2):** ε **floats** — LIFT picks it by *minimizing the memory-access count* `L(ε)` (Eq 6), which depends on `K_leaf` → data-dependent, **no fixed ceiling.** The `ε < 64` I saw is an *empirical observation* of where the cost-min landed on their datasets (used only to linearize `E = δεd`, Eq 8), **not a designed clamp.** → LIFT is the **"structurally can't have a worst-case bound"** branch → my fixed-envelope is a *fork they never took*, not a patch. **Precision (don't overclaim):** at *build* time PLA enforces ε as a hard max-error for the cost-chosen ε → their leaves *are* ε-bounded at construction; they just never **maintain** it under updates (density drifts → `E = δεd` moves → nothing re-bounds). **Build-time bound ✓; maintained-under-updates invariant ✗ — and that gap is the whole thesis.** (Full per-paper notes: `lift-notes.md`.)

**[DECISION — DEFERRED to build-time] Concurrency: ready-by-construction; prove single-threaded FIRST.** *Committed sequencing (my call):* prove the `c·m` theorem and demonstrate the **single-threaded** core first; concurrency is **designed-for but evaluated-later**, not part of the core result. Field precedent: PGM's core was single-threaded; **ALEX+, LIPP+, SALI were separate concurrent works** (only XIndex/FINEdex were natively concurrent). Don't headline concurrency before building it; *do* note the readiness. Full step-by-step engineering analysis lives in `concurrent-design-analysis.md` — deal with the machinery at build time.

- *Honest reframe (localizes the cost to the concurrency layer):* **pointer-free holds for the single-threaded theory — Hard Part 1 stands** (pure contiguous / PMA). *Adding* concurrency costs **one indirection** — a small (≤ 2m-entry) **mapping table** as the atomic publish layer (Bw-tree pattern). So it's "one indirection vs a pointer-*chain*": computed routing + 1 table lookup + 1 data access = **2 cache misses, depth-independent** (vs pointer trees' `depth` misses; vs pure-PGM's 1-but-no-publish-point). The indirection is a *concurrency-layer* tradeoff, **not** a core-layer one — so it doesn't touch the theory.
- *Architecture (one line, parts all validated):* computed routing → **mapping-table CAS-publish** for structural changes → **OLC** lock-free reads (→ **OptiQL**, PACMMOD 2023, under write contention — vanilla OLC retry-storms) → **epoch-based GC** → **background merge** (I6). Proven parts: ART (OLC), Bw-tree (mapping table), and — killing my own earlier "PMA is concurrency-hostile" worry — **parallel PMA (PPCSR, ALENEX 2021) + CPMA (PPoPP 2024)**, where a pointer-free PMA beats cache-optimized trees on *both* batch inserts (3×) and range queries (4×).
- ⭐ **THEORY HOOK 1 — merge/delete is the crux on BOTH axes (same frontier).** The open theorem (silent reducibility — a delete makes a pair mergeable with *nothing firing*) **is** the hard concurrency case (a merge cascade holds a *growing* locked region — exactly why B-tree *delete*-concurrency is harder than split). → **Proving the theorem and solving the concurrency are one effort, not two.** Another reason deletes are the whole game.
- 🟡 **THEORY HOOK 2 — `c·m` → bounded-contention corollary (potential bonus result).** The bound caps locked-region size → an adversary can't force unbounded serialization or unbounded mapping-table-rebuild frequency → **bounded worst-case contention under adversarial load**, a claim no learned index makes. Hypothesis, not proven; a bonus theorem to attempt *after* the main `c·m` result.
- *Red flags (committed with eyes open):* merge/delete cascade = the 🔴 hard cell (= the theorem itself); the contention corollary is 🟡 unproven; OLC reads need OptiQL to survive adversarial write contention. Everything else (routing, lookup, insert, split, GC, scans) is 🟢–🟡, assembled from validated parts. **All of this is build-time engineering — parked until the math is done.**

## 8. Enhancements & alternatives (secondary — detail behind the 🟡 Ledger rows)

### 8a. Per-segment adaptive transform `G` (I7) — optional space mode
Fit `pos = A·G(k) + b`, `G` a cheap monotone transform chosen per segment from a menu.
- **Preserves the machinery (proved):** convex hull runs identically in `(G(k), p)`-space (G warps the x-axis; ε lives on the y-axis/positions — untouched); constraint stays linear in (A,b); `G(sorted)` still sorted; free lemma holds in `G`-space; greedy stays **optimal for count** (union of prefix-closed predicates is prefix-closed). Build O(m·n), single-pass; 2-bit tag/segment.
- **⭐ Safety net — worst case = PGM:** `G=x` always in the menu → fancy `G` chosen only if it reaches ≥ as far → **segment count ≤ plain-PGM, always** (pure Pareto improvement on count). Safe; only operational costs.
- **Menu (hot-loop eval cost):** ✅ `x², x³`, low-deg poly, bit-shifts (~1–3 cyc); ✅ **cheap log via bit tricks** — `log₂≈` highest-set-bit (`clz`/`BSR`, ~1–3 cyc) or float-bits-as-int (fast-inv-sqrt family) — monotone, log-shaped, dirt cheap, *works because `G` needs shape+monotonicity not accuracy* (ε unaffected, R5) → **heavy-tailed data reachable cheaply**; 🟡 `√x` (~10–20 cyc); ❌ exact `ln/exp/trig` (~20–50 cyc, but bit-trick removes the need).
- **Cost-benefit:** ✅ index size **down** (main win, the R6 axis) · 🟡 lookup ~neutral (search still `log ε`; `G`-eval + branch can self-cancel — **don't sell as a lookup win**) · ❌ build ~m× · ❌ **merge harder** (m-way union check — taxes Hard Part 2) · ❌ breaks count=latency (R7) · ↔️ guarantee same (PGM floor).
- **Verdict:** minor space-reclamation, lookup-neutral, safe, complicates merge → **NOT core**; reserved mode, activate only if space is a *measured* problem. Keepers: the transform-agnostic proof, the bit-trick-log unlock, and the discipline of scoping it out.
- **8a-Tier2 (I8):** cost-aware selection ("use pricey `G` only if it saves ≥ threshold") — safe (PGM floor) but adds a knob + workload dependency + breaks the clean count proof. A **fixed threshold beats a tunable λ**; and excluding expensive transforms leaves little to adjudicate. **Default skip.**

### 8b. Connected spline + per-piece `G`, greedy (I9) — documented alternative
Spline (shared knots → ~1 number/piece → smaller) + per-piece `G`, **greedy not optimal**.
- **Going greedy fixes the build:** optimal+continuity+`G` → coupled DP (I13, fatal). Greedy → single-pass O(m·n), incremental. Recovers what optimal-spline killed.
- **Residual tension (R2) — pinned halves weaken free-split:** disjoint segments give the split-half *full re-fit freedom* → free lemma guarantees local feasibility. A spline pins the split-half at its shared knot → one less DOF → fitting (old ∪ new ∪ pinned-knot) within ε is harder, *sometimes infeasible* → needs more pieces or neighbor propagation (non-local). Structural to continuity; greedy-vs-optimal doesn't change it.
- **Clean tradeoff:** disjoint = strong guarantee, more space · spline = weaker guarantee, less space. Both single-pass.
- **Verdict:** **default = disjoint** (the free-split lemma is foundational — bedrock of I1/I3; trading "guaranteed local split" → "usually local" for bytes is bad for the *dynamic* core). Spline+`G`+greedy is a legitimate alternative — good for a *static*/space-critical variant, possibly fine dynamically **if pinned-half infeasibility is rare on real data** → **empirical question for the prototype.** Keep both; pick by measurement.

## 10. Why lines + L∞ is the right primitive (alternatives settled) + the residual dial

*Researched the question "is there a non-geometric / better way to break up the CDF?" across approximation theory, linear algebra, and synopsis literature. Verdict: lines + L∞ is the **unique cell** meeting all four of my needs, and the geometry I use is already the linear-algebra method.*

**Geometric = linear algebra (the false dichotomy).** The L∞-optimal line *is* a linear program: `min δ s.t. |yᵢ − (a·xᵢ + b)| ≤ δ`. My convex hull is its efficient geometric **dual** (the slab-intersection feasibility test); minimax/Chebyshev theory (the Alternation Theorem) is *built on* LP duality. So "is there a linear-algebra way instead of geometry?" — the geometry already **is** the LP. The one genuinely different algebraic path is changing the **norm**: least-squares (normal equations / QR / SVD) minimizes L₂ (average) → **no worst-case bound** (= RMI/ALEX emergent error). The hard per-key bound *requires* L∞ = the LP = the hull.

**The alternatives, settled (4 axes; each loses exactly one thing I need).** Test: hard per-key L∞ bound + cheaply maintainable + free-split lemma + automatic monotonicity.
- **Richer pieces** (polynomial/spline — RadixSpline, RMI-cubic) → fewer segments, but **lose the free lemma** (connected sub-range not independently valid), optimality → DP/nonconvex, monotonicity not free. (= my I12/I13 dead-ends, now externally confirmed.)
- **Different basis — wavelets / histograms** (the real "non-geometric" answers): wavelets = orthonormal basis, B-largest-coeffs = **optimal L₂** (genuine linear algebra, a matrix transform) but **lose L∞** (max-error wavelets are a hard separate problem). Histograms = piecewise-*constant*; max-error histograms exist (Guha–Shim) and the literature's "**robust histogram**" (adding buckets barely helps) = **my irreducibility under another name** — but constant pieces are strictly worse than lines for a CDF.
- **Transform the input — normalizing flows (NFL, VLDB'22):** learn a monotone bijection to near-uniform, then fit linear models; good empirical P99. **This is my `G` (I7) generalized to a global learned flow** — `G⁻¹∘F` is the ideal transform, NFL approximates it with a monotone net, my `G` is the cheap **bounded** per-segment case. NFL gains worst-case *empirically* but **loses the hard bound** (learned net, must be maintained).
- **Verdict:** lines + L∞ is the **only point** where worst-case-optimal + cheap + free-lemma + monotone *all* hold. Not settling — the unique feasible cell. Every alternative trades exactly one away.

**The proven floor (ammunition + honest framing).** Two lower bounds say PLA has a fundamental ceiling, so the `c·m` relaxation leaves no achievable optimality behind:
- *2025* (arXiv **2506.20139**): **Ω(κ·ε²)** lower bound on expected segment coverage for any ε-PLA fitter (κ data-dependent) — a floor on how few segments are possible.
- *2026* (arXiv **2601.06629**): stitch rescaled **x²** copies over M intervals → **any** K-segment PLA has error **Ω(1/K)** on that Lipschitz-CDF family. The deep statement: *lines have a fundamental ceiling on curved/adversarial CDFs, no algorithm escapes it.*
- **Use:** *ammunition* — my relaxation isn't leaving achievable optimality on the table (the optimum itself is bounded away from perfect); *honest framing* — marks exactly where lines can't win (high-curvature/adversarial) = where `G`/NFL earns its keep by removing curvature *before* the lines see it.

**⭐ NEW DIAL — residual shaping (I14).** The ε-bound caps the *max* |residual|; it says **nothing** about the *distribution* of residuals inside [−ε, +ε]. The feasible set for a segment is a **polytope** `{(a,b): max-error ≤ ε}`, not a point — the L∞-optimal (min-δ) line is one vertex, but *any* point is a valid ε-segment, and min-δ's equioscillation actually **pushes** residuals out to ±δ. Picking a feasible line whose residuals **cluster near 0** shortens the *typical* last-mile search (cost ≈ `log|residual|` for exp-search, + SIMD scan width) at **fixed ε and fixed segment count**. **The reframe:** minimizing `m` optimizes *space/height*; minimizing residual-spread optimizes *lookup speed* — **different objectives I had been conflating.** (The field already saw the symptom: OptimalPLA minimizes count but has *larger* intra-segment residuals than greedy.) Three knobs reshape residuals, all *inside* the hard envelope: (a) which feasible line — the LP-polytope choice; (b) `G` (x-axis warp); (c) accept marginal count/ε slack. **Right metric = residual histogram / probe-count, NOT segment count.** Caveats: *performance* refinement only (doesn't touch the theorem); build/refit-time (updates drift the shaping → re-shape, not an invariant); gain is data-dependent (no gain on already-concentrated data).

**Speculative thread — LP as a maintenance tool (sits next to cascade-bounding).** Since the fit is an LP, **warm-starting** (start from the prior optimal vertex; an insert/delete adds/removes one constraint → pivot the few steps to the new optimum) could cost work ∝ *how much the solution moved*, not segment size — which the convex-hull rebuild doesn't naturally give. The minimax **dual** (Lawson / interior-point on the Lq-weighted dual) is often stable under small input changes even when the primal jumps. Both this and the cascade-bounding lemma ask the *same* question — *how far does one edit propagate?* (the LP vertex within a segment ↔ boundaries between segments). **File as:** a lens to try *if* the convex-hull approach proves clumsy under mid-range edits — not a committed direction.

## 11. Adjacent research directions (good ideas, explicitly NOT this thesis)

*Parked here = sound, but serves a different goal (usually average-case performance) than this thesis's worst-case-guarantee contribution. Distinct from the 🔴 ledger dead-ends (which are reasoned-out-wrong); these are reasoned-out-**off-target**. Revisit only if the project's goal changes.*

**A1. RadixSpline (radix / computed) fast-path over the ε-segments — for an average-case-latency thesis, not this one.** *Idea:* a radix table (top-`r`-bits → array index → neighborhood) as an O(1) computed front-end over the bounded routing; common-case lookups skip the predict-then-search descent. RadixSpline's front end on PGM's back end — a coherent engineering point. *Why it's good (different goal):* on friendly/real-world distributions it shaves navigation to ~1 computed jump + 1 miss. *Why NOT this thesis (three reasons):* **(1) no worst-case value** — a radix table's quality is a function of key-spread across the *key space*; skew/duplicates (= Yang's exact attack ammunition) collapse it to "one slot owns everything," so it gives **zero** uplift in the adversarial regime the theorem targets — an average-case tool in a worst-case thesis. **(2) Taxes `c·m`** — I'm *updatable*, so a radix slot points into a *moving* segment structure; every split/merge needs radix-consistency maintenance → enlarges the cascade-bounding lemma's proof surface, and I'd pay that maintenance in exactly the adversarial case where the radix gives no benefit (cost in the analyzed case, benefit only in the un-analyzed case). **(3) Redundant** — I already have pointer-free recursive routing, so the marginal win is a constant-factor shave on already-bounded navigation, best-case only, while the fixed ε-leaf-search cost is untouched. *Reconsider iff* the goal shifts to "fastest average-case updatable learned index on real data" — then it's a sensible separate project (tuned + benchmarked, not proven). *Rule of thumb that generalizes:* a fast-path that helps only in the friendly case is fine in an average-case **system** and a liability in a worst-case **thesis** — doubly so when the structure is updatable, because then the fast-path isn't free to maintain.

## 12. Closing the latency gap with ALEX — engineering paths (performance, NOT the theorem)

*Where the gap is (from the cache/CPU analysis): **navigation already favors me** (computed, depth-independent ~2 misses, branch-predictable vs ALEX's depth-many pointer-chase stalls — and the win GROWS with data); the **only** place ALEX wins is the **leaf last-mile on friendly data** (its least-squares model often lands in 1–2 probes; my convex-hull fit can leave larger typical residuals). The gap is localized to one spot; these levers target it. All preserve the ε-bound; none touches the `c·m` theorem. Goal = competitive on reads, close on writes, decisively better on tail latency + under attack — NOT "match ALEX average-case."*

- **L1 — Residual shaping (I14): the direct hit on the gap.** The leaf last-mile *is* the gap, and the gap is typical-residual. Pick a feasible line (within the ε-polytope) that clusters residuals near 0 instead of the min-δ line that pushes them to the edge → typical probe count drops toward ALEX's 1–2. Same ε, same count. The one lever aimed at exactly the one thing ALEX does better.
- **L2 — SIMD the bounded window.** The ≤2ε window is fixed-width + contiguous → scan it in one vector compare. ALEX's exponential search *jumps* → not cleanly vectorizable. Converts "more probes" into "fewer, wider, vectorized scans" — a structural edge of a *bounded* window.
- **L3 — Prefetch the data block during model eval.** Computed routing knows the block address from arithmetic *before* it's needed → issue the prefetch while finishing the leaf model eval → the miss hides behind compute. ALEX can't (pointer-chase doesn't know the next address ahead). Free latency I get *because* routing is computed.
- **L4 — Cache-line / page-align blocks + co-locate the occupancy bitmap.** Lay out each block so one access pulls model + bitmap + ε-window together; minimize cache lines on the access that matters.
- **L5 — Keep the mapping table cache-resident.** ≤2m entries (tiny); pin it hot → the one indirection is an L1/L2 hit, not a memory round-trip → **erases the "extra indirection vs PGM" downside** entirely.
- **L6 — Tune fanout (shallow hierarchy) + PMA density (like ALEX's 0.6–0.8).** Trade a hair of routing precision for fewer levels; tune block density for the read/write mix (denser = better scan locality, sparser = cheaper inserts).
- **L7 — Radix / computed fast-path (the asterisked lever, see §11/A1).** Would let me *beat* ALEX on navigation (O(1) jump on friendly data), preserves the bound (degrades to bounded routing under skew). BUT taxes `c·m` (a moving radix table needs maintenance → enlarges the cascade-bounding proof surface) + average-case-only → **phase-2 / only-if-needed.** Almost certainly unneeded since navigation *already* favors me.

**Honest bottom line:** close the navigation gap entirely (already ahead) + most of the leaf gap (L1–L4), all within the ε envelope. What I *won't* fully match is ALEX's deepest cost-model adaptivity on pathological-but-real data — small residual, and chasing it is exactly the trade that costs the guarantee. Frame success as "small bounded average-case premium for a guarantee ALEX provably can't offer," NOT "matches ALEX average-case."
